Mentorship/Outreach

Mentorship of REU and SRCCS Students

During my tenure as a graduate student in the SRF group at Cornell University, I had the opportunity to mentor four undergraduate students. These students were part of either the Research Experience for Undergraduates (REU) program or the Summer Research for Community College Students (SRCCS) program. I was responsible for coming up with a research project and helping the student complete it during a summer. The programs provided a great opportunity for undergraduates to get exposed to graduate school-like research. It was also a great chance for me to give back to Cornell since I was lucky enough to participate in the REU program during the summer of 2009.

The REU and SRCCS programs gave me a great chance to learn about mentorship and teaching. I was given the freedom to develop a research program for my students and was the main person that they talked to while carrying out the project. Many of the students had not been in a research environment before. In addition to guiding them through their projects, I helped them learn about scientific writing and presentations as they had to write a final report and give a presentation at the end of the summer.



Matt Zotta was part of the SRCCS program in the summer of 2012. He was tasked with developing software for temperature mapping data collection. His project served as the basis for my own software that I developed later in my graduate career.

Ryan French was part of the SRCCS program in the summer of 2013. His project had two parts: first to develop a fast data acquisition system for the temperature mapping system, and second to design a new cavity testing insert for the temperature mapping system. His work greatly improved our temperature mapping capabilities which led to new scientific discoveries not only in my own work but in other graduate student's work at Cornell.


Nicolai Giedraitis was part of the SRCCS program in the summer of 2014. For the summer he focused on studying nitrogen diffusion into niobium. He conducted theoretical work and measured nitrogen diffusion using sample analysis techniques such as XPS and SIMS. His work directly contributed to my own project on nitrogen-doping by starting much of the sample analysis that I continued later. He also developed the basis for a nitrogen-diffusion model that was later continued by another student and then myself.

Waverly Gorman was a student in the REU program during the summer of 2015. Her project focused on continuing the theoretical work on nitrogen diffusion in niobium that Nicolai began in 2014 and helping to carry out cavity testing on the Cornell Horizontal Test Cryomodule as part of the LCLS-II High Q project. Her work helped pave the way for me to develop a full working diffusion model that could be applied to future nitrogen-doping experiments.
